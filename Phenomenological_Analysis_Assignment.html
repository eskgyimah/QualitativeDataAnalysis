1. INTRODUCTION

This assignment critically examines how Faraji and colleagues (2024) applied phenomenological analysis principles in their study exploring nurses' lived experiences caring for "recommended patients" - individuals admitted through personal connections or recommendations. The paper employs Colaizzi's seven-step descriptive phenomenological method, adhering to established guidelines for rigorous qualitative inquiry.

Phenomenological analysis seeks to understand how individuals experience and interpret specific phenomena, with meanings being made rather than found (as discussed in course materials). This assignment demonstrates how the authors implemented core phenomenological principles throughout their research process.

2. PHENOMENOLOGICAL PRINCIPLES APPLIED

2.1 FOCUS ON LIVED EXPERIENCE

Principle:

Phenomenology centers on capturing participants' firsthand experiences of a phenomenon, seeking to understand the essence and meaning from their perspective.

Application in the Paper:

The authors explicitly stated their aim was to "explain the experiences of nurses in caring for recommended patients" and to understand "the meaning and nature of their experiences." They positioned the study to explore lived experiences as perceived and interpreted by participants themselves, not predetermined by theoretical frameworks.

Evidence from Methodology:

  • Research question focused on subjective experience: "What are nurses' experiences in caring for recommended patients?"
  • Data collection used open-ended initial question: "Can you tell me about your experience of caring for recommended patients?"
  • This broad, non-directive approach allowed nurses to describe their experiences in their own words without researcher-imposed categories

Demonstration:

The authors successfully captured lived experience by selecting participants who had direct, prolonged engagement with the phenomenon (minimum 3 years clinical experience). The resulting themes - catastrophe, decline, progress, discrimination, work overload, and poor prognosis - emerged from nurses' actual experiences rather than theoretical predictions.

2.2 BRACKETING AND REFLEXIVITY

Principle:

Phenomenological researchers must acknowledge their own backgrounds, biases, and preconceptions, then "bracket" (set aside) these assumptions to see the phenomenon through participants' eyes. This reflexive exercise shapes how meanings are made.

Application in the Paper:

While the paper does not extensively detail bracketing procedures, the authors demonstrated reflexivity by:

  1. Acknowledging researcher backgrounds: The first author, who conducted interviews, held a master's degree in nursing and had clinical experience - potentially influencing interpretation
  2. Using established framework: Adopting Colaizzi's systematic method helped minimize subjective bias
  3. Member checking: Returning findings to participants allowed nurses to verify researchers' interpretations matched their intended meanings

Demonstration:

The authors recognized that as healthcare professionals, they might hold assumptions about patient care dynamics. By using semi-structured interviews with minimal prompting and validating interpretations with participants, they attempted to ensure findings reflected nurses' perspectives rather than researcher projections.

2.3 SYSTEMATIC DATA ANALYSIS PROCESS

Principle:

Phenomenological analysis follows rigorous, transparent procedures to extract meanings from raw data and develop coherent themes representing the phenomenon's essence.

Application in the Paper:

The authors applied Colaizzi's seven-step method systematically:

Step 1: Familiarization

  • Researchers read all interview transcripts multiple times
  • Gained holistic understanding of each participant's experience

Step 2: Extracting Significant Statements

  • Identified statements directly relevant to the phenomenon
  • Extracted 110 initial concepts from transcripts

Step 3: Formulating Meanings

  • Interpreted significant statements to uncover implicit meanings
  • Example: Statement "I had to prioritize recommended patients" was interpreted as reflecting "discrimination" and "work overload"

Step 4: Organizing into Themes

  • Grouped formulated meanings into six main themes
  • Ensured themes captured essential structures of experience

Step 5: Comprehensive Description

  • Developed exhaustive description of phenomenon
  • Integrated all themes into coherent narrative

Step 6: Identifying Fundamental Structure

  • Reduced description to essential components
  • Articulated core essence of caring for recommended patients

Step 7: Returning to Participants

  • Validated findings with nurses
  • Ensured interpretations resonated with lived experiences

Demonstration:

The authors demonstrated transparency by explicitly naming their analytical framework and using MAXQDA 2020 software to manage data systematically. The progression from 110 initial concepts to six themes shows rigorous data reduction while maintaining fidelity to participants' voices.

2.4 TRUSTWORTHINESS AND RIGOR

Principle:

Phenomenological research must establish credibility, dependability, conformability, and transferability to ensure findings are trustworthy.

Application in the Paper:

The authors applied Lincoln and Guba's evaluative criteria:

Credibility (truth-value):

  • Prolonged engagement: Interviews lasted 30-60 minutes, allowing depth
  • Member checking: Participants validated interpretations
  • Rich, thick description: Detailed quotes illustrated each theme

Dependability (consistency):

  • Audit trail: Clear description of analytical steps
  • Consistent methodology: Colaizzi's method applied uniformly across all transcripts

Conformability (neutrality):

  • Multiple researchers reviewed data independently
  • Reflexivity reduced researcher bias
  • Raw data available for verification

Transferability (applicability):

  • Detailed participant characteristics provided (demographics, experience levels)
  • Contextual information about setting (Kermanshah hospitals)
  • Readers can assess applicability to their contexts

Demonstration:

By explicitly addressing these four criteria, the authors showed commitment to phenomenological rigor. For example, providing verbatim quotes like "I was afraid because of medical errors that would cause death" demonstrated credibility through participants' authentic voices.

2.5 PARTICIPANT SELECTION AND PURPOSIVE SAMPLING

Principle:

Phenomenological studies require participants who have directly experienced the phenomenon under investigation and can articulate their experiences.

Application in the Paper:

The authors used purposive sampling with specific inclusion criteria:

  • Minimum 3 years clinical nursing experience
  • Direct experience caring for recommended patients
  • Willingness to share experiences

Demonstration:

This strategic selection ensured participants possessed rich, meaningful experiences. The 12 nurses represented diverse perspectives (58% women, 42% men) while sharing the common phenomenon. Sample size aligned with phenomenological traditions prioritizing depth over breadth.

2.6 IN-DEPTH, UNSTRUCTURED DATA COLLECTION

Principle:

Phenomenology requires open-ended, conversational data collection methods that allow participants to express experiences freely without constraint.

Application in the Paper:

The authors conducted semi-structured, in-depth interviews:

  • Face-to-face format enabled rapport and non-verbal observation
  • Audio recording captured exact wording
  • Open-ended initial question invited narrative responses
  • Flexible follow-up questions explored emergent themes
  • Interview duration (30-60 minutes) allowed comprehensive exploration

Demonstration:

Starting with "Can you tell me about your experience..." gave nurses freedom to emphasize aspects most meaningful to them. This contrasts with structured questionnaires that impose researchers' priorities. The resulting data revealed unexpected themes like "catastrophe" and "poor prognosis" that might not emerge from predetermined questions.

2.7 RICH, CONTEXTUAL DESCRIPTION

Principle:

Phenomenological findings should provide thick description that captures the phenomenon's texture, complexity, and contextual nuances.

Application in the Paper:

The authors presented findings with:

  • Extensive direct quotations preserving participants' language
  • Contextual details about hospital settings and patient dynamics
  • Multi-layered themes reflecting complexity (both negative and positive experiences)
  • Integration of individual variations within common themes

Example:

The theme "Be in progress" included nurses describing positive aspects: improved skills, professional growth, ethical satisfaction. This nuanced portrayal avoided oversimplification, showing the phenomenon's contradictory nature.

Demonstration:

By presenting six distinct yet interconnected themes, the authors captured the multifaceted reality of caring for recommended patients. This holistic representation aligns with phenomenology's commitment to understanding phenomena in their full complexity rather than reducing them to single variables.

3. CRITICAL EVALUATION

Strengths:

  1. Methodological Transparency: Clear articulation of Colaizzi's steps enables replication and evaluation
  2. Participant Validation: Member checking enhanced credibility
  3. Rich Data: Direct quotes illustrated themes powerfully
  4. Ethical Consideration: Obtained ethics approval and informed consent
  5. Contextual Grounding: Detailed setting description aids transferability

Areas for Enhancement:

  1. Limited Reflexivity Discussion: More explicit bracketing procedures would strengthen phenomenological rigor
  2. Researcher Positionality: Deeper exploration of how researchers' nursing backgrounds influenced interpretation
  3. Philosophical Grounding: Could strengthen by explicitly linking to phenomenological philosophers (Husserl, Heidegger, Merleau-Ponty)
  4. Negative Case Analysis: Discussion of disconfirming evidence would enhance trustworthiness

4. CONCLUSION

Faraji and colleagues (2024) effectively applied core phenomenological principles in their study of nurses' experiences caring for recommended patients. Their systematic use of Colaizzi's method, commitment to capturing lived experience, and attention to trustworthiness criteria demonstrate rigorous phenomenological inquiry.

The study exemplifies how phenomenological analysis makes meanings rather than finds them - researchers constructed interpretations collaboratively with participants through iterative reflection. The resulting themes provide actionable insights for healthcare policy while honoring nurses' subjective realities.

This analysis reveals that successful phenomenological research requires:

  • Disciplined adherence to established analytical frameworks
  • Reflexive awareness of researcher influence
  • Prioritization of participants' perspectives over theoretical impositions
  • Transparent documentation enabling external evaluation
  • Commitment to capturing phenomena's essential structures

The paper serves as a valuable model for conducting descriptive phenomenological studies in healthcare contexts, balancing methodological rigor with sensitivity to human experience.
