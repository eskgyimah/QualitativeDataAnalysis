1. INTRODUCTION

This assignment critically examines how Shali and colleagues (2024) applied ethnographic analysis principles in their focused ethnographic study exploring the culture of patient care among international nursing students in Iranian healthcare settings. The research demonstrates comprehensive ethnographic methodology through prolonged field engagement (52 observation sessions across three academic terms), participant observation, and systematic cultural analysis.

Ethnographic analysis, as emphasized in course materials, provides holistic description of shared culture, attitudes, values, norms, practices, patterns of interaction, perspectives, thoughts, emotions, and language of social/cultural groups. This assignment demonstrates how the authors systematically applied these principles to uncover and record variations in how international nursing students understand and enact patient care behaviors within a cross-cultural clinical context.

2. ETHNOGRAPHIC ANALYSIS PRINCIPLES APPLIED

2.1 HOLISTIC CULTURAL DESCRIPTION

Principle:

Ethnography requires comprehensive, holistic description that captures the complexity and interconnectedness of cultural phenomena, avoiding reductionist fragmentation.

Application in the Paper:

The authors adopted focused ethnography to provide integrated understanding of multiple cultural dimensions simultaneously:

Cultural Domains Examined:

1. Shared Values and Attitudes:

  • Respect for patient dignity across cultures
  • Commitment to cultural competence development
  • Recognition of universal caring principles
  • Acceptance of cultural diversity in healthcare

2. Practices and Patterns of Interaction:

  • Clinical training procedures (dressing, medication administration)
  • Student-patient communication patterns
  • Student-instructor interactions
  • Peer learning dynamics among international students
  • Cultural adaptation strategies in clinical settings

3. Perspectives and Thoughts:

  • Students' conceptualizations of culturally sensitive care
  • Philosophical orientations toward difference and commonality
  • Cognitive frameworks for navigating cultural challenges
  • Meaning-making processes around cross-cultural encounters

4. Emotions:

  • Anxiety about cultural misunderstandings
  • Pride in cultural identity
  • Frustration with language barriers
  • Satisfaction from successful cultural bridging
  • Empathy development through cultural immersion

5. Language:

  • Linguistic challenges in patient communication
  • Translation strategies employed
  • Development of medical terminology in non-native languages
  • Non-verbal communication adaptations

Demonstration:

Rather than isolating single aspects, the authors presented an integrated cultural model showing how cognitive dimensions (cultural competence acquisition), behavioral dimensions (patient care practices), and affective dimensions (emotional responses to difference) interweave to constitute the culture of patient care. This holistic lens reveals that international students don't merely learn patient care skills but become enculturated into a complex professional culture requiring simultaneous navigation of medical, linguistic, social, and ethical domains.

The overarching cultural model—acquisition of cultural competence through accepting differences and finding commonalities—captures this holistic essence by showing how students synthesize diverse cultural elements into coherent professional identities.

2.2 PROLONGED FIELD ENGAGEMENT

Principle:

Ethnography requires sustained immersion in the field to develop deep cultural understanding, observe patterns over time, and build trust with participants.

Application in the Paper:

The authors demonstrated exceptional commitment to prolonged engagement:

Temporal Scope:

  • Duration: February 2019 to August 2021
  • Timeframe: 3 complete academic terms (approximately 2.5 years)
  • Total observations: 52 separate sessions
  • Total observation hours: 50 hours
  • Session length: 30-90 minutes per observation

Depth of Engagement:

This extended timeline enabled researchers to:

  • Observe students across different stages of clinical training
  • Track cultural learning trajectories over time
  • Witness seasonal and contextual variations
  • Build rapport reducing observer effects
  • Recognize patterns invisible in brief encounters

Demonstration:

Unlike "helicopter ethnography" involving brief site visits, the authors' 2.5-year commitment exemplifies authentic ethnographic immersion. The 52 observation sessions distributed across three terms ensured exposure to varied clinical scenarios, different patient populations, and evolving student competencies.

This prolonged engagement was essential for identifying the cultural process of "acquisition of cultural competence"—a developmental trajectory only visible through longitudinal observation. Short-term observation might capture isolated behaviors, but the extended fieldwork revealed how international students progressively integrated into Iranian healthcare culture while maintaining distinct cultural identities.

The timeframe also allowed researchers to observe recurring patterns that constitute "culture" rather than idiosyncratic individual behaviors. Repeated observations of similar situations (e.g., multiple medication administration events) enabled distinction between personal variation and shared cultural practices.

2.3 PARTICIPANT OBSERVATION

Principle:

Ethnographers participate in the social world they study while simultaneously observing, balancing insider and outsider perspectives.

Application in the Paper:

The authors conducted systematic participant observation across multiple clinical activities:

Observation Activities:

1. Clinical Training Observation:

  • Monitored students during hands-on patient care
  • Observed skill acquisition in real clinical contexts
  • Watched instructor demonstrations and student responses

2. Procedural Observation:

  • Dressing procedures: Wound care, bandage changes
  • Medication administration: Drug preparation, dosage calculations, patient education
  • Vital signs monitoring: Temperature, blood pressure, pulse measurement
  • Case file examination: Documentation practices, charting behaviors

3. Interaction Observation:

  • Student-patient conversations
  • Student-instructor consultations
  • Peer discussions among international students
  • Interprofessional interactions with hospital staff

4. Informal Discussions:

  • Casual conversations during breaks
  • Impromptu exchanges in clinical settings
  • Post-procedure debriefings

Participant-Observer Role:

The researchers, themselves nurses and instructors, occupied an optimal position for participant observation:

  • Insider Status: Professional nursing background enabled understanding of clinical nuances
  • Outsider Perspective: Research role maintained analytical distance
  • Trusted Position: Instructor status facilitated student openness
  • Cultural Bridging: Iranian researchers observing international students created productive cultural tension enabling insight

Demonstration:

The authors' dual identity as nurse-instructors and ethnographers exemplifies the participant-observer continuum. Their professional expertise allowed recognition of subtle clinical behaviors meaningful to practitioners but potentially invisible to lay observers. For example, observing how students adapt dressing techniques to accommodate religious modesty requirements requires clinical knowledge to distinguish technical from cultural modifications.

Simultaneously, adopting the researcher stance enabled questioning taken-for-granted practices that insiders might overlook. The research lens prompted examination of why certain patient care approaches are culturally normalized while others are contested.

The informal discussions integrated into observation sessions demonstrate the ethnographic insight that culture emerges not only through formal actions but through off-stage conversations, jokes, and casual remarks revealing underlying values and assumptions.

2.4 THE FIVE ASPECTS OF BEHAVIOR INTERPRETATION

Principle:

Ethnographic analysis interprets five key aspects: (1) Processes of behavior, (2) Products/outcomes of behavior, (3) Philosophies surrounding behavior, (4) Motivations, and (5) Cultural foundations of behavior.

Application in the Paper:

The authors systematically addressed all five aspects:

ASPECT 1: PROCESSES OF BEHAVIOR

How Patient Care Behaviors Occur:

Cultural Learning Process:

  • Step 1: Initial cultural shock and confusion
  • Step 2: Recognition of differences
  • Step 3: Active observation and imitation
  • Step 4: Experimentation with adaptation strategies
  • Step 5: Integration of home and host culture practices
  • Step 6: Development of hybrid cultural competence

Acceptance of Differences Process:

  • Acknowledging cultural diversity in healthcare expectations
  • Suspending judgment of unfamiliar practices
  • Seeking understanding through inquiry
  • Adjusting care approaches contextually

Finding Commonalities Process:

  • Identifying universal human needs (pain relief, dignity, comfort)
  • Discovering shared professional values across cultures
  • Recognizing common biological realities transcending culture

Demonstration:

The authors revealed that cultural competence acquisition isn't a linear skill development but a cyclical process involving ongoing negotiation between difference and commonality. International students don't simply "add" Iranian practices to their repertoire but engage in complex cultural translation, determining when to prioritize host culture norms, when to maintain home culture approaches, and when to create innovative hybrids.

For example, students from cultures emphasizing family involvement in patient care learned to navigate Iranian protocols while advocating for family-centered modifications when culturally appropriate.

ASPECT 2: PRODUCTS/OUTCOMES OF BEHAVIOR

Results of Patient Care Cultural Learning:

Individual Outcomes:

  • Enhanced cultural competence
  • Expanded clinical skill repertoire
  • Increased cultural humility
  • Professional identity transformation
  • Personal growth through cultural immersion

Interpersonal Outcomes:

  • Improved patient-student rapport across cultures
  • Reduced cultural misunderstandings
  • Enhanced peer learning through cultural exchange
  • Strengthened multicultural teamwork

Institutional Outcomes:

  • Diversified nursing workforce
  • Enriched clinical education through international perspectives
  • Institutional cultural competence development
  • Cross-cultural knowledge production

Patient Care Outcomes:

  • Culturally sensitive care delivery
  • Improved patient satisfaction
  • Reduced health disparities
  • Enhanced therapeutic relationships

Demonstration:

By documenting tangible outcomes, the authors showed that the culture of patient care among international students produces measurable consequences extending beyond individual learning to affect patients, institutions, and healthcare systems. The outcome of "avoiding cultural bias" demonstrates that cultural competence isn't merely additive knowledge but transformative consciousness reshaping how students perceive and enact care.

ASPECT 3: PHILOSOPHIES SURROUNDING BEHAVIOR

Beliefs and Worldviews About Patient Care:

Underlying Philosophies Identified:

Universalism:

  • Belief in common human dignity across cultures
  • Recognition of shared professional ethics
  • Faith in biology as transcultural common ground

Cultural Relativism:

  • Acceptance that care practices vary legitimately across cultures
  • Respect for diverse health beliefs and practices
  • Rejection of cultural hierarchy or superiority

Pragmatic Eclecticism:

  • Philosophy that best care draws from multiple cultural traditions
  • Openness to learning from host culture
  • Willingness to adapt practices contextually

Patient-Centeredness:

  • Belief that patient cultural preferences should guide care
  • Commitment to individualized rather than protocol-driven care
  • Philosophy that cultural competence serves patient welfare

Demonstration:

The authors uncovered implicit philosophical tensions international students navigate. Some expressed tension between relativist respect for Iranian practices and personal ethical commitments from home cultures. For instance, students from cultures emphasizing patient autonomy sometimes philosophically questioned paternalistic care approaches observed in Iranian settings, requiring negotiation between professional socialization and personal values.

The philosophy of "finding commonalities" reflects an integrative worldview rejecting both uncritical cultural assimilation and rigid cultural separatism. Students philosophically positioned themselves as cultural bridges, synthesizing multiple traditions to create transcultural professional identities.

ASPECT 4: MOTIVATIONS (Economic, Social, Psychological)

Why Students Engage in Cultural Learning:

Educational Motivations:

  • Degree completion requirements
  • Clinical competency attainment
  • Professional certification goals

Professional Motivations:

  • Career advancement through international credentials
  • Desire to practice nursing globally
  • Aspiration to serve diverse populations
  • Enhancement of employment competitiveness

Social Motivations:

  • Building cross-cultural relationships
  • Gaining international experience
  • Expanding social networks
  • Developing cosmopolitan identities

Psychological Motivations:

  • Curiosity about different cultures
  • Personal growth and self-discovery
  • Challenge-seeking and adventure
  • Overcoming cultural discomfort
  • Proving adaptability and resilience

Ethical Motivations:

  • Commitment to equitable care regardless of culture
  • Desire to reduce health disparities
  • Moral obligation to understand patients holistically
  • Dedication to social justice in healthcare

Economic Motivations:

  • International education as investment in human capital
  • Access to employment opportunities unavailable in home countries
  • Potential for higher earnings with international qualifications

Demonstration:

The authors recognized that student behavior stems from complex motivational webs. A single behavior—for example, intensively studying Persian medical terminology—might simultaneously serve educational requirements, professional aspirations, social integration, and psychological needs for competence.

The motivational analysis revealed that cultural learning isn't merely externally imposed (curriculum requirements) but intrinsically motivated by students' identity projects. International students actively sought cultural challenges as opportunities for self-transformation, not just obstacles to overcome.

ASPECT 5: CULTURAL FOUNDATIONS OF BEHAVIOR

Why Individuals/Groups Behave Based on Culture:

Home Culture Foundations:

  • Previous socialization in different healthcare systems
  • Cultural values from countries of origin (Ghana, Nigeria, Iraq, Afghanistan, Ethiopia)
  • Religious beliefs influencing care practices
  • Traditional healing knowledge from home communities
  • Cultural communication styles and norms

Host Culture Foundations:

  • Iranian healthcare system structure and protocols
  • Persian language and communication patterns
  • Islamic cultural values in Iranian context
  • Iranian concepts of family, authority, and respect
  • Local health beliefs and practices

Professional Culture Foundations:

  • Global nursing professional standards
  • Biomedical paradigm shared across nations
  • Evidence-based practice norms
  • Universal nursing ethics (beneficence, non-maleficence, autonomy, justice)

Educational Culture Foundations:

  • Academic expectations of Iranian nursing programs
  • Clinical training pedagogies
  • Assessment and evaluation criteria
  • Formal and hidden curricula

Demonstration:

The authors showed that international students navigate at least four cultural systems simultaneously: home culture, host culture, professional nursing culture, and educational culture. Behavior emerges from the intersection and sometimes collision of these cultural foundations.

For example, a student from Ghana administering medication in an Iranian hospital draws on:

  • Ghanaian cultural norms about patient-provider relationships
  • Iranian hospital protocols for medication administration
  • Universal nursing principles of medication safety
  • Instructors' specific educational expectations

Cultural competence acquisition involves learning which cultural foundation takes precedence in different situations—a complex cultural calculus requiring sophisticated cultural intelligence.

The cultural foundations analysis revealed that students don't merely "adapt" to Iranian culture but engage in creative cultural bricolage, assembling new hybrid cultural practices from multiple source traditions.

2.5 THE FOUR STAGES OF ETHNOGRAPHIC ANALYSIS

Principle:

Ethnographic analysis proceeds through: (1) Detecting and interpreting thematic categorizations, (2) Searching for inconsistencies and contradictions, (3) Generating conclusions about events, and (4) Providing explanations to occurrences.

Application in the Paper:

The authors employed Roper and Shapira's five-step inductive ethnographic content analysis, which encompasses all four ethnographic stages:

STAGE 1: DETECTING AND INTERPRETING THEMATIC CATEGORIZATIONS

Application:

  • Step 1-2 (Transcription and Initial Coding + Sorting Patterns):
    • Transcribed all interviews and field notes
    • Coded raw data for recurrent patterns
    • Sorted codes into preliminary categories

Thematic Categories Detected:

  • Cultural differences in patient care expectations
  • Language and communication challenges
  • Adaptation strategies employed by students
  • Instructor support and guidance
  • Peer learning and cultural exchange
  • Clinical skill acquisition processes
  • Emotional responses to cultural encounters

Demonstration:

The authors moved from granular observations ("Student from Nigeria hesitated when male patient requested physical exam") to conceptual categories ("gender norms affecting patient care") to overarching themes ("accepting differences").

This thematic detection wasn't mechanical coding but interpretive work requiring researchers to understand cultural meanings. For instance, recognizing that a student's silence during a clinical procedure might indicate cultural respect (in some contexts) or confusion (in others) required deep cultural interpretation.

STAGE 2: SEARCHING FOR INCONSISTENCIES AND CONTRADICTIONS

Application:

  • Step 3 (Identifying Outliers):
    • Actively sought disconfirming cases
    • Examined contradictions within and across participant accounts
    • Explored variations that didn't fit emerging patterns

Inconsistencies Explored:

Individual Variation:

  • Some students rapidly acquired cultural competence; others struggled persistently
  • Variable comfort with cultural adaptation
  • Differing strategies for managing cultural dissonance

Contextual Contradictions:

  • Behaviors appropriate in one clinical setting inappropriate in another
  • Temporal inconsistencies (practices acceptable during earlier training phases but not later)

Value Conflicts:

  • Tension between home culture values and host culture expectations
  • Conflicts between personal beliefs and professional requirements
  • Contradictions between stated philosophies and enacted behaviors

Demonstration:

Rather than presenting a tidy, uniform picture, the authors acknowledged cultural complexity and ambiguity. For example, while the overarching theme emphasized "finding commonalities," some students expressed frustration that differences sometimes felt insurmountable.

This attention to contradiction enhances credibility by demonstrating that findings emerged from data complexity rather than selective reporting confirming preconceptions. The authors recognized that culture isn't a harmonious, consistent system but a contested terrain where actors negotiate competing demands and values.

STAGE 3: GENERATING CONCLUSIONS ABOUT EVENTS

Application:

  • Step 4 (Generalizing Constructs):
    • Synthesized patterns across observations and interviews
    • Developed conceptual conclusions transcending specific events
    • Constructed cultural model explaining observed phenomena

Conclusions Generated:

Overarching Cultural Model:

"Acquisition of cultural competence through acceptance of differences and finding commonalities"

Component Conclusions:

1. Cultural Competence is Acquired, Not Innate:

International students aren't naturally culturally competent but develop competence through intentional learning processes.

2. Dual Processes are Essential:

Cultural competence requires both accepting differences (respecting uniqueness) AND finding commonalities (identifying shared humanity).

3. Clinical Context Shapes Cultural Learning:

Patient care provides unique cultural learning opportunities unavailable in classroom settings.

4. Peer Diversity Enriches Cultural Education:

International student cohorts create "third spaces" where multiple cultural perspectives interact.

Demonstration:

The authors moved from descriptive observation ("Students from different countries used varied patient communication approaches") to analytical conclusion ("Cultural competence develops through dialectical engagement with both cultural difference and commonality").

These conclusions answer the "so what?" question, transforming ethnographic description into conceptual insights applicable beyond the specific Iranian context. The conclusion that cultural competence requires balancing acceptance of difference with recognition of commonality has universal relevance for multicultural healthcare education globally.

STAGE 4: PROVIDING EXPLANATIONS TO OCCURRENCES

Application:

  • Step 5 (Memo-writing):
    • Developed explanatory frameworks
    • Articulated "why" and "how" behind observed patterns
    • Connected findings to broader theoretical and practical contexts

Explanations Provided:

WHY International Students Successfully Develop Cultural Competence:

  • Prolonged immersion in host culture clinical settings
  • Intrinsic motivation to succeed in international education
  • Peer support from fellow international students
  • Instructor guidance facilitating cultural navigation
  • Cognitive flexibility developed through prior cultural transitions
  • High stakes of professional certification requiring adaptation

HOW Cultural Competence Acquisition Occurs:

  • Through progressive exposure to diverse patient populations
  • Via iterative trial-and-error in clinical practice
  • By observing and imitating culturally competent role models
  • Through reflective processing of cultural encounters
  • Via integration of feedback from instructors and patients
  • By developing metacognitive awareness of cultural assumptions

WHY "Accepting Differences AND Finding Commonalities" Framework Emerges:

  • Reflects pragmatic necessity: Students must respect Iranian norms (accept differences) while maintaining professional identity (find commonalities)
  • Represents psychological coping: Balancing difference and commonality reduces cultural overwhelm
  • Embodies professional nursing values: Patient-centered care requires both cultural specificity and universal caring

Demonstration:

The explanatory framework doesn't merely describe WHAT cultural competence looks like but explains WHY it develops through specific processes and HOW context shapes those processes.

The authors explained that international students' cultural learning success stems from a convergence of factors: individual motivation, institutional support, peer dynamics, and the inherently cross-cultural nature of contemporary healthcare. This multi-level explanation recognizes that cultural phenomena emerge from complex interactions between individual agency and structural contexts.

The explanation also addresses practical implications: Understanding HOW cultural competence is acquired informs curriculum design, suggesting that effective cultural education requires authentic clinical immersion, diverse peer cohorts, and reflective processing—not merely didactic cultural content.

2.6 THICK DESCRIPTION

Principle:

Ethnography employs "thick description" (Geertz) providing rich, detailed, contextual accounts that capture not just behaviors but their meanings, contexts, and interpretive frameworks.

Application in the Paper:

While BMC Nursing format constrains extensive narrative, the authors incorporated thick description elements:

Contextual Detail:

  • Specific clinical settings described (medical-surgical units)
  • Temporal contexts specified (academic terms, observation durations)
  • Cultural backgrounds enumerated (countries of origin, languages)
  • Procedural specifics noted (dressing changes, medication administration)

Meaning-Layer Descriptions:

Rather than stating "Students learned patient care," the authors described:

  • Initial anxieties and uncertainties
  • Gradual confidence development
  • Specific challenges encountered
  • Strategies devised to overcome barriers
  • Emotional trajectories through cultural learning

Demonstration:

Though constrained by journal format, the authors' descriptions enabled readers to visualize the cultural world of international nursing students. The thick description conveyed not just that students performed procedures but how those performances were culturally laden acts requiring navigation of multiple meaning systems.

For example, describing medication administration as involving not merely technical skill but cultural negotiation (reading Persian labels, explaining procedures in non-native language, respecting patient cultural preferences about medication timing) provides thickness revealing the cultural complexity of seemingly routine behaviors.

2.7 SYSTEMATIC DATA COLLECTION AND TRIANGULATION

Principle:

Ethnography employs multiple data sources to build comprehensive, verified understanding through triangulation.

Application in the Paper:

The authors collected data through multiple methods:

Data Sources:

1. Participant Observation (52 sessions, 50 hours):

  • Direct witnessing of behaviors in natural contexts
  • Real-time field notes
  • Observation of student-patient-instructor interactions

2. Semi-Structured Interviews (23 interviews):

  • In-depth conversations with 17 students and 4 instructors
  • Opportunity for participants to articulate meanings
  • Exploration of experiences not observable

3. Field Notes:

  • Observational notes documenting behaviors
  • Methodological notes about research process
  • Theoretical notes connecting observations to concepts

4. Memoirs:

  • Researchers' reflective writings
  • Documentation of insights and questions
  • Record of analytic development

5. Audio Recordings:

  • Verbatim capture of interviews
  • Preservation of exact wording and tone

Triangulation Strategy:

  • Data Triangulation: Multiple sources (students, instructors)
  • Methodological Triangulation: Observations + interviews + documents
  • Time Triangulation: Multiple observations across three terms
  • Spatial Triangulation: Different clinical settings

Demonstration:

Triangulation enhanced credibility by enabling cross-verification. For example, if observations suggested students struggled with language barriers, interview data confirmed or complicated that interpretation by revealing students' subjective experiences and coping strategies.

The combination of observational and interview data captured both enacted behaviors and espoused meanings, sometimes revealing gaps between what participants say they do and actual practices—a hallmark ethnographic insight.

2.8 REFLEXIVITY AND RESEARCHER POSITIONALITY

Principle:

Ethnographers must critically reflect on how their backgrounds, assumptions, and positions influence what they observe, how they interpret, and what they report.

Application in the Paper:

The authors demonstrated reflexivity by:

Acknowledging Positionality:

  • Identified themselves as nurses and instructors
  • Recognized insider status in nursing profession
  • Acknowledged potential biases from Iranian cultural immersion

Reflexive Methods:

  • Memoirs capturing self-awareness
  • Continuous examination of assumptions
  • Critical friend discussions
  • Member checking with participants

Epistemological Reflexivity:

  • Recognized that "culture" is researcher-constructed interpretation, not objective reality
  • Acknowledged selective attention inherent in observation
  • Understood that their presence influenced student behaviors

Demonstration:

The authors' reflexive awareness enhanced trustworthiness by showing they didn't naively assume objective neutrality but consciously managed subjective influences. Their status as Iranian nurse-instructors potentially biased them toward host culture perspectives, but reflexive awareness enabled compensatory attention to international students' alternative viewpoints.

Reflexivity also appeared in methodological choices: using multiple data sources and independent analysis mitigated individual researcher biases, while member checking allowed participants to correct misinterpretations.

4. CRITICAL EVALUATION

Strengths:

  1. Exemplary Prolonged Engagement: 2.5 years across three academic terms demonstrates authentic ethnographic commitment
  2. Systematic Methodology: Roper-Shapira five-step analysis provides transparent, replicable framework
  3. Cultural Relevance: Addresses contemporary global nursing education challenges
  4. Practical Significance: Findings directly inform multicultural healthcare education
  5. Comprehensive Data Collection: Multiple methods enhance credibility
  6. All Five Aspects Addressed: Systematic attention to processes, products, philosophies, motivations, cultural foundations
  7. Four-Stage Analysis: Complete progression from thematic detection through explanation
  8. Reflexivity: Critical self-awareness enhances trustworthiness
  9. Participant Diversity: 21 participants from multiple countries provide rich cross-cultural insights
  10. Focus on Underexplored Population: International nursing students' cultural experiences understudied

Limitations and Areas for Enhancement:

  1. Limited Thick Description: Journal format constraints reduce narrative richness compared to ethnographic monographs
  2. Iranian Context Specificity: Findings may not transfer to other host countries with different cultural dynamics
  3. Language Barriers: Research likely conducted in Persian and English, potentially excluding participants less fluent in either
  4. Observer Effects: Nurse-instructor researchers may have altered student behaviors due to evaluation concerns
  5. Sampling: Only students from specific countries included; broader diversity would enhance comprehensiveness
  6. Gender Dynamics: Gender composition of participants not fully explored as cultural variable
  7. Temporal Limitation: While 2.5 years is substantial, longer longitudinal tracking could reveal post-graduation cultural integration
  8. Power Dynamics: Instructor-student relationship may have inhibited critical comments about Iranian healthcare culture

5. INTEGRATION WITH COURSE CONCEPTS

This paper exemplifies key course principles about ethnographic analysis:

Holistic Description:

The study captured interconnected dimensions—values, practices, emotions, language—demonstrating that culture is integrated system, not collection of isolated traits.

Prolonged Field Engagement:

The 2.5-year timeline exemplifies ethnographic commitment to depth over breadth, prioritizing intensive understanding of one cultural context.

Five Aspects of Behavior:

Systematic attention to processes, products, philosophies, motivations, and cultural foundations demonstrates comprehensive cultural interpretation.

Four Analytical Stages:

Clear progression from thematic detection through contradiction exploration to conclusion generation and explanation provision shows rigorous ethnographic reasoning.

Cultural Patterns vs. Individual Variation:

The study balanced pattern identification (shared cultural competence acquisition processes) with recognition of individual differences, avoiding stereotyping.

Emic and Etic Perspectives:

Combined insider views (participants' meanings) with outsider analysis (researchers' theoretical interpretations), characteristic of ethnographic dual consciousness.

6. CONCLUSION

Shali and colleagues (2024) effectively applied ethnographic analysis principles in their focused ethnographic study of international nursing students' patient care culture. Their exemplary prolonged field engagement (52 sessions across 2.5 years), systematic participant observation, comprehensive five-aspect behavior interpretation, and rigorous four-stage analytical process demonstrate sophisticated ethnographic inquiry.

The study exemplifies how ethnographic analysis provides holistic description of shared cultural patterns, uncovering and recording variations in how different groups understand and enact behavior. By examining international students' cultural learning processes, the authors revealed that cultural competence acquisition involves complex negotiation between accepting differences and finding commonalities—a dialectical process only visible through sustained ethnographic investigation.

Most significantly, the research demonstrates ethnography's practical value beyond academic description. The finding that cultural competence develops through authentic clinical immersion, peer diversity, and reflexive processing informs nursing education globally, suggesting specific pedagogical approaches to enhance cultural competency.

This analysis reveals that successful ethnographic research requires:

  • Prolonged, intensive field engagement (not brief site visits)
  • Participant observation balancing insider and outsider perspectives
  • Systematic attention to processes, products, philosophies, motivations, and cultural foundations
  • Rigorous progression through detection, contradiction-seeking, conclusion-generating, and explanation-providing stages
  • Multiple data sources enabling triangulation
  • Thick description capturing cultural complexity and meaning
  • Reflexive awareness of researcher influence
  • Commitment to representing cultural patterns while honoring individual variation

The paper serves as an exemplary model for conducting focused ethnographic studies in healthcare contexts, demonstrating how cultural analysis can simultaneously advance theoretical understanding and practical application.
